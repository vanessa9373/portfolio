The 6R Cloud Migration Model in Practice

Apr 18, 2026 6 min read AWS Migration Cloud

The 6R framework is one of the most cited models in cloud migration — and one of the most misapplied. In theory, it's a clean decision tree: assess each workload and assign it a strategy. In practice, the lines between strategies blur, organizational politics complicate technical decisions, and the model gets treated as a formality rather than a genuine tool. Here's how I actually use it.

The 6 Strategies — What They Actually Mean

Rehost (Lift-and-Shift)

Move the workload to cloud infrastructure without changing anything about the application. Same OS, same application server, same database engine — just running on EC2 instead of on-prem VMware.

When it's the right call: Speed is the priority, the team lacks cloud skills, or the application is a black box with no available source code. Rehost is also strategically right as a first step when you need to exit a data center by a hard deadline. You can always modernize after the workload is in the cloud — you can't modernize while the data center is shutting down around you.

The hidden cost: Rehosted workloads often don't realize cloud economics. You're paying cloud prices for an architecture designed for on-prem economics. Plan a replatform or refactor pass within 12–18 months if the workload is important.

Replatform (Lift, Tinker, and Shift)

Make targeted optimizations during migration without changing the core architecture. Classic examples: swap self-managed MySQL on EC2 for Amazon RDS, move file storage from NFS to Amazon EFS or S3, replace Apache Tomcat with Elastic Beanstalk.

When it's the right call: The workload is worth investing in, there's a clear operational win available (like managed patching or automatic backups), and the team has bandwidth for a contained modernization effort. Replatform often delivers 20–30% cost savings over rehost with manageable risk.

Refactor (Re-architect)

Redesign the application to be cloud-native — breaking monoliths into microservices, moving to serverless, adopting managed data services. This is the highest ROI strategy and the highest risk.

When it's the right call: The workload is business-critical, the team has cloud maturity, and there's a clear functional gap that the current architecture can't address (scalability, developer velocity, cost at scale). I've seen refactors deliver 60–70% cost reductions for the right workloads — but I've also seen refactor projects balloon in scope and never ship. Scope tightly.

Repurchase (Drop and Shop)

Replace the existing solution with a SaaS product. Move your on-prem CRM to Salesforce, your email server to Microsoft 365, your VPN to Zscaler.

When it's the right call: The application is commodity infrastructure that doesn't differentiate your business, the on-prem version is expensive to maintain, and a mature SaaS alternative exists. Repurchase is often the highest-ROI decision for non-core systems — you eliminate maintenance overhead entirely.

The gotcha: SaaS migrations involve data migration, user retraining, and integration work that teams consistently underestimate. Budget for change management, not just the SaaS subscription.

Retire

Decommission applications that are no longer needed. This sounds simple, but it's politically difficult — someone always feels ownership of a system, even if no one is using it.

How I identify retire candidates: Pull the last-login or last-access logs for every application. Anything with no meaningful activity in 90 days is a retirement candidate. Have the business owner confirm the application is still needed — you'll often find it isn't. I've seen discovery processes surface 10–20% of an application portfolio as retire candidates, which significantly simplifies the migration.

Retain

Keep the workload on-prem, at least for now. This is the right answer for applications with hard compliance constraints (data sovereignty, air-gapped requirements), recent capital investment, or technical incompatibilities with cloud deployment.

Important: Retain is a decision, not a default. Applications end up in the retain bucket for legitimate reasons — and also because no one wanted to deal with them. Make sure retain decisions are documented with a review date, so they get revisited rather than forgotten.

How I Apply It in Practice

During a migration assessment, I score each application on two dimensions: migration complexity (technical risk, dependencies, team familiarity) and business value (revenue impact, user base, strategic importance). This produces a 2x2 that makes the 6R decisions much more intuitive:

  • Low complexity, low value → Retire or Retain
  • Low complexity, high value → Rehost first, replatform later
  • High complexity, low value → Repurchase or Retire
  • High complexity, high value → Refactor (with appropriate investment and timeline)

The most important thing the 6R model does is force explicit decisions. Every workload gets a strategy, every strategy gets a justification, and every stakeholder can see the reasoning. That transparency is what keeps migration programs on track when they get hard.
